Richard Konda, Executive Director of the Asian Law Alliance

Opening Statement from Tom Izu

Tom Izu is the Executive Director of the California History Center and the Audrey Edna Butcher Civil Liberties Education Initiative at De Anza College. He is also a board member of the Santa Clara Valley Chapter of the American Civil Liberties Union.

Many of us who have been active in the Japanese American community of the San Jose/Santa Clara Valley area have been quite concerned with the increasingly, hateful rhetoric being thrown about against Muslim Americans and others that is eerily reminiscent of the scapegoating and fear mongering our community faced so many years ago.

And it is especially upsetting for us to hear references during election campaigning, made about the World War II imprisonment of Japanese Americans, in a way that attempts to make it seem reasonable and justifiable to repeat a similar course of action that could lead to massive violations of civil liberties and rights of other individuals and entire communities.

We have invested too much time in organizing and educating the public about the lessons learned by our community during World War II, to see our country suddenly have amnesia about its own history.

Tonight, we first want to set the record straight and make sure all of us know the facts about the internment so our story is not used against others and against the reasons why we fought for and eventually won redress. Then, we ask each of you to help stand up against scapegoating and fear mongering and use this history and the stories we wish to share to inspire you to do so.

Richard Konda, Executive Director of the Asian Law Alliance

The incarceration by the US government of the Japanese American community has been thoroughly repudiated – this is important to understand when the uninformed and ignorant attempt to distort historical facts and cite the incarceration as good public policy.

  • When Congress passed the Civil Liberties act of 1987 – members of Congress on behalf of the nation stated that the purpose of the act was to acknowledge and apologize for the fundamental injustice of the evacuation, relocation and internment of Japanese Americans. When President Ronald Reagan signed the Civil Liberties act on August 10, 1988, he stated, “We gather here today to right a grave wrong.” He further stated, “For here we admit a wrong”
  • During World War II, the U.S. Supreme Court failed to protect civil liberties in the cases of Fred Korematsu and Gordon Hirabayashi. However, the failure of the Court was not caused by the quality of the arguments of the Government attorneys - but because these same government attorneys suppressed key evidence and altered government documents.
    This misconduct by these government attorneys was unethical and criminal. In the 1980s when this misconduct came to light – Fred Korematsu and Gordon Hirabayashi’s convictions were vacated.
  • In 2011, the U.S. Solicitor General – whose office argued the Korematsu and Hirabayashi cases at the Supreme Court confessed to error and mistakes made in both cases.
  • Many individuals who were involved in the internment later deeply regretted their roles – Justice William O. Douglas – a member of the US Supreme Court in the Korematsu case stated that the evacuation case, “was ever on my conscience.” Chief Justice Earl Warren, who at the time of the internment was the governor of California stated, “I have since deeply regretted the removal order and my testimony advocating it.”

Asian Law Alliance stands together with the other organizations here tonight in speaking out against hate crimes, scapegoating and fear mongering.
