Arizona SB1070 and Japanese Americans

 

What is Arizona's SB 1070?

SB 1070 was signed into law by Republican governor Jan Brewer at the end of April. The law goes into effect on July 29, 2010. SB1070 makes it a state crime to be in the United States without proper authorization. It authorizes local police to arrest individuals where there is a "reasonable suspicion" that they are unauthorized immigrants, and allows individuals to sue the police if they feel that the law is not being enforced.

SB 1070 was widely opposed by Chicanos and Native Americans in Arizona, who have called for a national boycott of Arizona. In response, local activists across the country have condemned the law as racist. They have pressured local governments to criticize the law and some localities have supported the boycott. In early July, the Federal government filed suit to overturn the law and get an injunction to prevent it from being enforced.

Why do opponents of the law call it racist?

Opponents of SB 1070 say that the law will lead to racial profiling and point out that a number of native-born Chicanos have been arrested by police for not carrying documentation that identifies them to be authorized to be in the United States.

Why the call for the boycott?

Tourism is one of the largest industries in Arizona. In the 1990's, the NAACP and other African American and civil rights groups called a boycott of Arizona because of Arizona's refusal to recognize the Martin Luther King Junior birthday as a holiday.  In response, the Super Bowl was moved to California. Eventually, Arizona recognized the date as a holiday.

Why did the Federal government file suite against SB 1070?

The U.S. Constitution and Supreme Court cases have established that the Federal government is in charge of immigration and not the states. For example, California Proposition 187, which required local schools to collect immigration documentation, was overturned in Federal courts. Further, SB 1070 goes further than Federal laws, since it is not a crime to be in the U.S. without authorization (it is a civil offense, punishable by deportation, but not jail time).

Why should Japanese Americans and other Asian Americans be concerned about SB 1070?

The authors of SB 1070 see it as a first step towards restricting legal immigration and stripping citizenship from native-born children whose parents are unauthorized. Restrictions on legal immigration (such as the 2007 proposal to eliminate family reunification visas) would have a huge impact on Asian Americans. The proposal to take citizenship away from native-born children was tried with the Chinese Americans. This was fought in the courts all the way to the U.S. Supreme Court, who ruled in 1898 in the United States v. Wong Kim Ark that all native-born are citizens, as established in the 14th Amendment to the Constitution and English common law. SB 1070 is also like the California Anti-Alien Land Law, in that it makes no mention of race, but is clearly aimed at the non-white communities.
