Hidden Internment:
The Art Shibayama Story


2-4 pm, September 13, 2009
Yu-Ai Kai Building
588 N. Fourth Street,
San Jose, CA 95112

Event Website
 
Hidden Internment reveals the lesser-known history of Japanese Latin American rendition through the life story of Isamu Carlos Arturo "Art" Shibayama, who was taken from Peru on March 22, 1944 and incarcerated in Crystal City, Texas.  
  

From December 1941 to February 1948, the U.S. government orchestrated and financed the mass abduction, forcible deportation and incarceration of 2,264 men, women, and children of Japanese ancestry from 13 Latin American countries. The U.S. planned to use them as hostages in exchange for Americans held by Japan. Over 800 Japanese Latin Americans were included in two prisoner of war exchanges between the U.S. and Japan.

After the short film presentation at this event, you will be able to hear Art Shibayama discuss his experience.

  
   
    At this event, the Japanese American Museum of San Jose (JAMsj) will also have some images of the Crystal City camp, where Art Shibayama was incarcerated, on display. Not only were Japanese Latin Americans and Japanese Americans imprisoned there, the camp also held several hundred people with German ancestry and at least one Italian Latin American family.
 		  
   
"As we entered the town of Crystal City, we remembered seeing the large statue of Popeye in the middle of town. A feeling of apprehension overcoming us at the sight of the 10 foot high cyclone fence topped with barbed wire that encircled the camp."
  From "The Moritas (Takuritsu)" by Tetsuko Morita Suzuki. Crystal City 50th Anniversary Reunion Album. Editor: Joy Nozaki Gee, Crystal City Association, 1993.

Light the Night for Peace and Friendship
 
 
   

In loving memory of 9/11 victims

Light the Night for Peace and Friendship

 

  
         
The American Muslim Voice Foundation, a strong supporter and participant in our annual San Jose Day of Remembrance event and a Peace Partner with NOC,  will host a memorial in memory of 9/11 victims in Washington D.C (for more info: AMV website).  
     
From the American Muslim Voice website:  
     
  How can we help one another to forgive, heal old wounds and sow the seeds of peace and friendship? Hold a candlelight vigil after sunset, honor 9/11 victims and their families. The vigils may be held in your front yard, neighborhood parks or places of worship. Or just light a candle, put it on your window  
and pray for the victims, their families and for peace in our world.

On Sept 11th, open your home or places of worship to old friends and future friends. Share your vision  of peace and take the step to achieve that goal.

Spirit of Japantown Festival
Saturday, September 26, 2009

Festival website: www.spiritofjapantownfestival.com
 
San Jose Japantown is one the last three major Japantowns that remain in the United States. This festival highlights the diverse culture and traditions of this historic district.

NOC is proud to announce that famed Origami Grand Master, Jemma Jio (the daughter of NOC Treasurer, Gary Jio), will be giving lessons on origami, the traditional Japanese art of folding paper into various decorative shapes, in the NOC booth.  Come by our booth and pick-up your free NOC Crane of Peace from Jemma.

Japanese American Resettlement Through the Lens
 
 

Author Book Presentation and Book Signing

Japanese American Resettlement
Through The Lens
 

6:00 pm, October 20, 2009
Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. Library
Room 229/225

 

  
       
Author and UCLA professor, Dr. Lane Hirabayashi, will be discussing his book, Japanese American Resettlement Through the Lens: Hikaru Iwasaki and the WRA's Photographic Section, 1943-1945. Japanese American Resettlement through the Lens contains a unique collection of photographs by War Relocation Authority photographer Hikaru Iwasaki, the last surviving WRA photographer from that period.
