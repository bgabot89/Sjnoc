30th Annual Day of Remembrance
 


30th Annual San Jose
Day of Remembrance

"Redress/Reparations: Unfinished Business"

Sunday, February 14, 2010
5:30pm-7:30pm
San Jose Buddhist Church Betsuin
640 North Fifth Street,
San Jose, CA

Free and open to the public

 

 

  
   
 

The San Jose Day of Remembrance commemorates the anniversary of  Executive Order 9066 which led to the forced incarceration of 120,000 people of Japanese descent, two-thirds of whom were American citizens.

   
  Traditional candlelight procession through San Jose's historic Japantown. Photo courtesy of Andy Frazer.   A candle is lit for each of the camps. Photo courtesy of Andy Frazer.

Thirty years ago, NOC established the San Jose Day of Remembrance event to bring awareness to the plight of former Japanese American incarcerees and to build support for efforts in getting an official apology from the United States government. At the time, there was not widespread support for redress and reparations, even within the Japanese American community, but eventually the redress movement gathered momentum. Education and legislative efforts from many organizations and individuals culminated in the signing of the Civil Liberties Act of 1988.

As we look back on that landmark legislation, we also acknowledge that there were many who were similarly affected by the incarceration  but were not given the same official apology from the U.S. government. In a form of rendition, the U.S. government orchestrated and financed the mass abduction, forcible deportation and internment of 2,264 men, women, and children of Japanese ancestry from 13 Latin American countries. Most Japanese Latin Americans were excluded from the Civil Liberties Act of 1988 because the U.S. government claimed they were “illegal aliens.”

Similar in spirit to the early Day of Remembrance programs, the 2010 Day of Remembrance program will describe grassroots and legislative efforts in establishing a commission that would investigate and determine the facts surrounding the wartime deportation and incarceration of Latin Americans of Japanese descent by the U.S. government. The struggle for a  formal acknowledgement for this great injustice reminds us that that there is still much unfinished business related to the Civil Liberties Act of 1988.


  

The Day of Remembrance is also an event that aims to bring different communities together in order to build trust, respect and understanding among all people  The event allows all of us to renew our pledge to fight for equality, justice and peace.

As we come together once again for our annual event, we are cognizant that we are still in the midst of two wars. We have recently witnessed over the last few weeks calls for racial and religious profiling and we are concerned about a potential backlash that could fracture our community. The unfinished business of the Civil Liberties Act informs us that we must always be vigilant in protecting our community against prejudice and war hysteria.

This year's Day of Remembrance program also features special performances by San Jose Taiko and shakuhachi master, Dick Matsueda, Congressman Mike Honda, speakers from the community, and the traditional candlelight procession through historic San Jose Japantown.

  
San Jose Taiko will make their annual appearance at the Day of Remembrance.
